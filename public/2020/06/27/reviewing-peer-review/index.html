Reviewing Peer Review

2020/06/27

I was once asked to a review a paper on classical (digital) superconducting electronics. I’m not really an expert on that kind of thing, but I know enough to be dangerous. After reading the paper I couldn’t detect any obvious flaws. I didn’t have access to their data, so clearly I couldn’t check that they actually got the results they claimed, but I could check consistency between tables and figures, words and figures, and so on. After a few read-throughs I felt I understood what the authors had done, and that it was likely correct. What I couldn’t fathom was why. In particular, I thought they could have accomplished their goal with a much simpler (and faster) device. Nor did I really understand how why the work was necessary at all. Was this grounds enough for rejection? My instinct was yes, but I had not really acted as an official reviewer before this point. One of the side-effects of moving straight from academia to industry and switching fields at the same time is that you are not well known enough to be on anyone’s list. I decided to consult with one of my much more experienced co-workers. His advice, to paraphrase, was that the journal editor decides whether the work is interesting and I decide whether it’s bullshit.

I ended up suggesting that it be published with revisions, and that the authors should better develop the context and motivation for their work.

In the end the whole experience left me feeling uncertain about whether I’d done a good enough job and apprehensive about whether the process had been useful for the authors at all. I’m not convinced that, had I been a true superconducting digital logic expert, I would have been able to better evaluate their work. At least not without access to the data and the analysis. Of course, I wouldn’t have wanted to sink that much work into a review without gaining some tangible benefit from it!

The problems with peer-review, especially in the social sciences, have been belabored at length. If you don’t believe me, just search for #peer-review on Twitter to get a taste.
